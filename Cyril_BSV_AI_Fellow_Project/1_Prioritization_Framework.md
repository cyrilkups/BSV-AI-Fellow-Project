# Prioritization Framework

I ranked the companies based on where I saw the strongest combination of painful developer workflow, AI-native timing, infrastructure potential, adoption path, and defensibility. I was not looking for the most interesting demo. I was looking for products that could become part of the daily operating stack for teams building or deploying AI systems.

## Criteria
1. Severity of pain
   Does the product solve a problem that teams already feel acutely?
2. AI-native urgency
   Is the category becoming more important because of the shift toward production AI systems?
3. Technical leverage
   Does the product benefit from deep technical execution rather than shallow feature bundling?
4. Workflow embedding
   Can it become part of a repeated workflow instead of a one-off utility?
5. Adoption friction
   Is the path to trying and adopting the product realistic for the target user?
6. Defensibility
   Could this compound into a durable product layer, community, or data advantage?
7. Fit with BSV's thesis around AI improving productivity
   Does the company sit close to a real productivity gain rather than vague AI aspiration?

## Ranked List

### 1. BAML
BAML is the clearest fit with the thesis I find most compelling right now: production AI breaks at the interface between messy model outputs and normal software systems. Typed LLM interfaces, schema enforcement, prompt testing, and reliability tooling all map to real engineering pain. If BAML becomes part of how teams define and ship LLM features, it can sit very close to the application development workflow.

### 2. Mem0
Memory infrastructure matters more as agents move from single-turn novelty toward longer-lived workflows. The product sits on an important layer of the stack, but the space could get crowded quickly and may become dependent on what model providers bundle natively. I like the direction, but I want sharper conviction on how durable the moat becomes.

### 3. Tigris Data
Tigris is interesting because developer-facing storage and data infrastructure remain foundational, especially as AI workloads push teams toward globally available systems with simpler APIs. The challenge is that infra markets attract strong incumbents and pricing pressure fast. This feels important, but I would need to believe the wedge is more than "better cloud storage."

### 4. Parasail
Parasail is intriguing if it truly improves how teams buy, schedule, or manage compute in AI-heavy environments. Compute pain is real, but this is a market where margins, reliability, and differentiation matter a lot. I would want to spend time understanding whether the product is becoming workflow-critical or just economically convenient.

### 5. Atuin
Atuin has real developer love and strong workflow embedding, which I respect. The product feels sticky for power users because terminal history is one of those tiny but constant pains that compounds over time. My hesitation is whether that love expands into a venture-scale platform or stays a sharp but narrower productivity tool.

### 6. Primitive
Primitive could be meaningful if it changes how developers understand large codebases and onboard into unfamiliar repositories. That is a real pain, especially as code volume and AI-generated code increase. I rank it lower because I would want proof that the product earns repeated use rather than brief curiosity.

### 7. Rasa
Rasa has enterprise relevance and clear history in conversational AI, which makes it credible. The issue for me is that it feels less tied to the newest developer workflow shift around building AI-native software, and less early than some of the other companies here. It may still be a good business, but it is not where my strongest curiosity goes first.

### 8. Entire
Entire ranks last because product clarity and developer-tool relevance feel less obvious from the public surface area I reviewed. That does not mean it is weak. It means I had less immediate confidence about the pain point, the buyer, and why this would become a must-have product in my preferred part of the market.
